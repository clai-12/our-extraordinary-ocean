Sunlit Zone

Spanning from 0 to 200 meters deep, the sunlit zone is the uppermost layer of the ocean where sunlight penetrates fully. This is also home to a vast proportion of the sea's biodiversity!

Ocean Sunfish

Mola mola

Size: About 6 feet (long and tall!)

Habitat: temperate and tropical waters of each ocean.

Discovery Date: As early as 1758

Living mainly in the sunlit zone, the ocean sunfish, or Mola mola, is one of the strangest fish in the sea. On top of being the heaviest bony fish in the world, its atypical shape further sets it apart from its peers.

Despite their sunny name, (given to them because of their “basking” behavior on the surface,) the ocean sunfish actually spends much of its life hunting for food in the depths. Their basking behavior may also help attract seabirds and cleaner fish that remove parasites, which they carry in large numbers.

Although often seen drifting near the surface, sunfish are capable of diving hundreds of meters in search of food before returning upward to warm themselves again. Despite its size and intimidating appearance, the sunfish eats mostly soft prey like jellyfish. Due to their low-calorie diet, the sunfish has to spend much of its time hunting to support its large frame and energy usage.

While they are sometimes mistaken for great whites due to their fins protruding from the surface of the water, sunfish are gentle giants and slow swimmers who are often preyed on by orcas, seals, and sharks. However, their thick skin (up to 3 inches!) and few nerve endings help them survive these attacks. In some cases, seals have been observed to bite sunfish for sport, abandoning them after a large bite rather than eating them immediately.

European Eel

Anguilla anguilla

Size: 18 to 26 inches

Habitat: Rivers, lakes, estuaries, and the open ocean.

Discovery Date: Ancient

The European Eel, Anguilla anguilla, has been a staple in scientific research since ancient times — and many of its reproductive mysteries remain unsolved today. Our knowledge of the eel has been informed by many of the Western world’s top minds; Aristotle himself proposed that the eel originated from the mud, while Sigmund Freud dissected over 400 eels in his youth to find an answer.

How has the European Eel eluded science for so long? Its life cycle is unlike any other animal, and it wasn’t until relatively recently (late 19th century) that the eel’s life cycle was confirmed to include the many stages people already knew them as: glass eels (a delicacy in Spain), elvers, yellow eels and silver eels.

Another factor could have been its changing habitats. An eel spends much of its adult life in freshwater, like rivers, lakes, or even family wells, before migrating out to sea to spawn. When yellow eels mature and transition into silver eels, their body changes color for better camouflage in the open ocean, and the eel stops feeding to prepare for the long journey. Their eyes also increase in size for better vision, and they develop gonads in preparation for spawning.

In 2022, their 3,700 mile journey to the Sargasso Sea was finally documented through satellite tracking.

Fun fact: The Brantevik Eel was a European Eel believed to have lived over 150 years in a Swedish well.

Midwater Octopus

Japetella diaphana

Size: 6 inches

Habitat: Midwater, where they are completely open.

Discovery Date: 1885

The Japetella diaphana is a small octopus that lives in the midwater zone of tropical and subtropical oceans. It lives in open water, far from the seafloor, where there is no physical cover and very little light. Since it lives so deep, most information about it comes from deep-sea nets or submersibles, so many parts of its life cycle are still not fully understood.

To survive in the open water, one of its most important adaptations is bioluminescence. This midwater octopus has light-producing organs on its underside that help it match faint light coming from above. This makes it harder to see from below, especially for predators looking up toward the surface.

Additionally, the Japetella diaphana can change its body color — much like many other cephalopods. It has been shown to change into a more orange or red color, as well as an almost-black, allowing it to better camouflage under varying light conditions.

We don’t know much about its reproduction specifically, but due to its midwater environment, we believe that female Japetella diaphana carry its eggs in open water rather than attaching them to rocks, which is more conventional for octopuses. This ups the stakes for their camouflage game, as they are also directly responsible for the survival of their young.

Sperm Whale

Physeter macrocephalus

Size: 60 feet

Habitat: Deep temperate and tropical waters.

Discovery Date: 1758 (formally)

Sperm whales are deep divers, and their diet consists mainly of large cephalopods, especially squid, which they locate using echolocation in near-total darkness. Scars from squid tentacles are often visible on their skin.

Despite their size and deep-ocean lifestyle, sperm whales are mammals and give live birth, with calves developing internally for over a year before being born and nursed with milk. This reproductive strategy sets them apart from most marine life and links them closely to other mammals despite their extreme aquatic adaptations.

Unlike many ocean species, its biology is well understood, partly due to its long history of interaction with humans through whaling.For centuries, sperm whales were hunted, primarily for their large head cavity filled with spermaceti oil, which was highly valued for use in lamps, lubricants, and industrial applications. This high demand made sperm whales a prime target for commercialized whaling.

Another valuable product associated with sperm whales is ambergris, a rare substance formed in the digestive system and occasionally expelled into the ocean. Ambergris has historically been prized in perfumery due to its ability to stabilize and enhance scents, although it is only found in a small percentage of sperm whales.

Fun fact: The sperm whale is the largest toothed predator on Earth and one of the most heavily studied deep-diving marine mammals

Midnight Zone

Spanning from 1,000 to 4,000 meters deep, the midnight zone exists in complete darkness with no sunlight penetration. Temperatures are low, and pressure is high. Animals often rely on bioluminescence for communication, camouflage, or attracting prey.

Vampire Squid

Vampyroteuthis infernalis

Size: 11 inches

Habitat: Deep temperate and tropical waters.

Discovery Date: 1898 to 1899

The vampire squid, Vampyroteuthis infernalis, is one of the ocean's oddest and most misunderstood creatures. Despite its name, “vampire squid from hell,” it is not a vampire, nor dangerous. In fact, it is not even a true squid. Instead, it is a distinct family of cephalopods, in which there are no other species.

Its name reflects its appearance when scientists first found it: a dark red or black body, large eyes, and a cloak-like webbing between its arms. In the deep sea, where the light is dim and blue is the only color that reaches far down, these features are necessary for survival. Its dark red skin appears almost black in deep water because red lightwaves are difficult to see in the deep.

Unlike many squids that hunt, the vampire squid feeds on “marine snow”—tiny bits of dead plants, animals, and waste that drift through the water like underwater dust. It uses two long, thin feeding filaments to collect this material. These filaments stretch out to grab food and then pull it back in when it’s done eating.

Like all cephalopods, its body is soft and jelly-like, with no hard bones or strong muscles. This design helps it withstand the extreme pressure of the deep ocean, where crushing forces would harm most animals. Because its body is so flexible and filled with water, it doesn’t get crushed.

If threatened, it can wrap its webbed arms around itself like a cloak and even flash small glowing lights at the tips of its arms, which may confuse predators and give it a chance to escape.

Anglerfish

Order Lophiiformes

Size: 4 feet

Habitat: Deep sea and seafloor.

Discovery Date: 1833 (though there are over 200 species!)

Anglerfish, order Lophiiformes, are a group of deep-sea fish known for their unusual appearance. (You may remember them from Finding Nemo!) Despite their cultural image, they’re just trying to survive.

There are many types of anglerfish, and they're not all the deep-sea “monster” from the movies. Some live in shallow coastal waters and don’t look as extreme as the deep-sea species. The deep sea anglerfish live mostly in the midnight zone, where food is extremely scarce. Because of this, almost everything about their biology is shaped by the need to attract prey in total darkness.

The most famous feature of the anglerfish is its bioluminescent lure. A modified spine on its head acts like a fishing rod, with a glowing tip produced by symbiotic bacteria. This light acts as bait in the dark ocean, attracting curious animals close enough for the anglerfish to strike. When prey comes within range, the anglerfish uses a sudden, fast mouth movement to swallow it whole.

Anglerfish are also known for extreme sexual dimorphism, meaning males and females can look completely different. Females are large, equipped with the glowing lure and built for hunting. Males, in contrast, are much smaller and do not have lures — their main role is reproduction. In many deep-sea species, males permanently attach themselves to females after finding them. Over time, the male fuses with the female’s body and becomes dependent on her for nutrients, acting mainly as a source of sperm when needed.

Abyssal Zone

Spanning from 4,000 to 6,000 meters deep, the abyssal zone is characterized by near-freezing temperatures and perpetual darkness. Food is hard to come by and typically arrives as organic material drifting down from upper layers.

Giant Isopod

Bathynomus giganteus

Size: 16 inches

Habitat: Seafloor, Indo West Pacific and Western Atlantic ocean

Discovery Date: 1879

The giant isopod, Bathynomus giganteus, is a deep-sea crustacean related to the small “woodlice” or pill bugs that are sometimes found under rocks and logs in your backyard. They move slowly across the ocean floor using their many legs, just like their land relatives. Though it looks quite different from them, an isopod is a type of crustacean like crabs, shrimp, and lobsters.

One of the most interesting evolutionary features of giant isopods is deep-sea gigantism, the tendency for some deep-ocean species to grow much larger than their shallow-water or land relatives. Instead of being a few centimeters long like a typical pill bug, giant isopods can reach over 30 centimeters. Scientists still debate why this happens, but likely explanations include slow growth in cold temperatures, long lifespans, and the energy-saving advantages of a low-metabolism lifestyle in a food-poor environment, which favor a lower surface area compared to its volume.

Giant isopods are scavengers. They feed on dead animals that sink to the seafloor, including fish, squid, and large carcasses like whales. Since food is so rare on the seafloor, the giant isopod has adapted to long periods without sustenance. When a food source does appear, such as through whale falls, they can feed quickly and store energy to survive these long gaps.

Tripod Fish

Bathypterois grallator

Size: 14 in ches (over 3 feet with the tripod fins!)

Habitat: Seafloor, temperate and tropical waters.

Discovery Date: 1886

The tripod fish is best known for its extreme fin modifications, or its “tripod”, which gives it an unusual way of life compared to most fish. Instead of swimming around, the tripod fish “stands” on the ocean floor!

In this position, it faces into the current, holding its body slightly above the ground. This elevation helps it catch small drifting organisms and organic particles carried by deep-sea currents. Rather than actively swimming or chasing prey, the tripod fish often remains still for long periods, functioning as an ambush feeder.

This lifestyle is closely tied to energy conservation. In the deep sea, food is scarce and encounters are unpredictable, so many organisms reduce movement to conserve energy. The tripod fish fits this pattern by minimizing activity and relying on existing feeding opportunities rather than hunting.

Reproduction in tripod fish is unusual compared to most vertebrates. Individuals are believed to be simultaneous hermaphrodites, meaning they possess both male and female reproductive organs at the same time. In some observations, self-fertilization has been suggested as a possible reproductive strategy when mates are not available, although this is still not fully confirmed in all populations.

Snailfish

Family Liparidae

Size: 20 inches

Habitat: Tide pools all the way down to 27,200 feet.

Discovery Date: 1861 (still discovering species in 2025!)

The snailfish is a group of deep-sea fishes in the family Liparidae, best known for including some of the deepest-living fish ever discovered. Certain species, such as those found in ocean trenches like the Mariana Trench, have been recorded at depths greater than 8,000 meters, making them among the deepest vertebrates known to science.

Snailfish have soft, gelatinous bodies that look almost translucent. They lack rigid skeletons, which helps them survive under extreme pressure: their bodies are adapted to match the surrounding water pressure, allowing them to swim fluidly instead of being crushed by the weight of the sea.

At these depths, food is extremely hard to find. As a result, snailfish feed on most anything they come by. They eat small invertebrates, such as amphipods, and organic material they can find drifting or resting on the seafloor. Their feeding strategy is based on taking advantage of whatever is available.

Reproduction in snailfish is less well understood, but like many deep-sea fish, they produce eggs that develop in extreme conditions, with early life stages likely drifting or living near the seafloor until maturity.

Sponges

phylum Porifera

Size: Incredibly varied by species.

Habitat: Various seafloors.

Discovery Date: ??

Sponges are some of the simplest animals — they do not have organs, muscles, or tissues. Instead, their bodies are full of tiny channels that allow seawater to flow through. As water passes through, microscopic food particles and bacteria are captured by specialized cells.

Deep-sea sponges are some of the longest-living animals on Earth. Some glass sponges in polar and deep waters have been suggested to reach ages of 10,000–15,000 years, although exact lifespans are difficult to measure because they grow so slowly.

There is also a wide diversity of deep-sea sponge forms. Glass sponges, for example, build delicate skeletons made of silica that can form intricate lattice-like structures, sometimes creating reef systems on the seafloor. Antarctic sponges are adapted to some of the coldest ocean waters on Earth, growing slowly in stable but freezing conditions. Giant barrel sponges, found in tropical deep waters, can reach enormous sizes and may represent centuries of continuous growth.

Fun fact: The largest ever sponge was 12 feet long, 7 feet wide, and 4.9 feet high.
